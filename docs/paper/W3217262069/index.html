The Challenges of delivering sustainability in urban development: A case study of the Low2No model

OpenAlex · en

Abstract

One of the biggest challenges that the cities of the 21st century face is how they can find solutions for sustainability that balance environmental, ecological and social dimensions. \nFor this challenge Sitra, the Finnish Innovation Fund, developed a replicable model of design and construction, LOW2No, which aims to deliver a more sustainable built environment and a foundation for sustainable lifestyles. \nThe model was first tested in .Jätkäsaari, Helsinki. \n\nBased on the first LOW2No project, the research aims to understand why delivering sustainability is challenging in urban development and proposes suggestions of how the model of sustainable urban development could be developed further. \nThe research was conducted as a qualitative single-case study in spring 2012. \nA total of 11 people involved in the project were interviewed and an extensive literature review was carried out. \n\nBased on the case study, the research found that the current urban development process is fragmented into two phases: the development phase and the use and maintenance phase. \nIn addition, the development organizations of the project were driven by their own goals, which often overruled the shared vision of the project. \nIn addition, existing operational models of the built environment are not supporting holistically sustainable solutions, which cause part-optimization in the process. \nIn addition the effectiveness of a project as an intervention for broader systemic change is very limited if a project cannot be connected to larger change. \nThe biggest challenge for LOW2No is that there are very few incentives to cut down carbon. \nCarbon reductions have to be proceeded indirectly through other issues, such as energy, which will make carbon always subordinate to other incentives. \n\nThe study proposes a network of critical actors for future LOW2No projects. \nThe LoW2No network would nest broader systemic change and direct development in the network. \nAt the project level, new shared business models should be developed that look at value from the perspective of the entire network. \nThese models would better capture the value that the business creates over the lifecycle and engage multiple actors with shared goals. \nTo have more effectiveness, future projects should be more refined and goals better communicated. \nThe findings of this study succeed in looking beyond immediate solutions and layout the initial steps towards a platform for more extensive systemic change. \nAs LOW2No is one of the world's leading projects to address the issue of sustainability in existing cities, the context places this thesis in a globally important discussion of how the cities of the 21&lt;sup&gt;st&lt;/sup&gt; century can be transformed for a sustainable future.
