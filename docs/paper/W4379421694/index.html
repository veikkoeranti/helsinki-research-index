MODERN militarybuildings in Finland are at last receiving the attention they deserve. Fouryears after Sirpa Haila's study of Russian barrackbuilding in the igios (reviewed in SEER, 75, 1997, PP. I76-78), Anne Makinen has published a significantand pioneering work on Finnish functionalistmilitary architecturefrom the mid I920S to the late I93os. The way the book draws together the relationshipsbetween architecture,political power, society and the militaryhierarchybroadensitsappealbeyond architecturalhistory.There is a usefulsummaryin English. Dissatisfactionwith the barracksdesigned by the National Board of Public Buildings prompted the Ministry of Defence to gain control over its own construction work in I926. The drawings and archive of the Construction Bureau of the Ministry's Technical Department constitute the author's principal sources. The Bureau became a major architectural practice, employing an unusually large number of women architects (a matter of particularinterest to the author), and applying modern ideas to the requirementsofthedefenceforces .Functionalismatthattimerepresentedadistinctive style offering a blend of hygiene, health, standardization, technology and REVIEWS 549 modernismwhich appealed to the defence forcesof the new Finnishrepublic, not least because it contrasted completely with the style of the red-brick barracksinherited from the Russians- everything Russian was bad. Whitepainted functionalistbuildings were symbolically attractive to the victorious armedforcesofpolitically'White'Finland.Moreover,theirhygienicconditions were an important weapon in the fight against tuberculosis (dangerously common in society and the army)and as a demonstrationof how the defence forcestookgood careof theirconscripts,a desirablepoliticalandsocialpoint. Independent Finland took over barrackscapable of housing over 6o,ooo men, ample for the defence forces'requirements,but many buildingswere in poor condition and some areas of strategic importance lacked barrack accommodation altogether.Although the Finnishdefence budget wasproportionately high by European standards,money was in short supply and new buildings had to be constructed economically. However, the Ministry of Defence was able to undertakea considerablebuildingprogramme although financial constraints had an effect on the architects' designs. The author concentratesparticularlyon the design and suitabilityof the barracksfor the Motor TransportCompany in Helsinki (built I934-35), the Training Centre at Niinisalo (I935), the air force bases at Kauhava and Immola, the military hospitals in Viipuri (Vyborg, I929-32) and Helsinki (I934-36), and the MilitaryAcademy at Santahamina(I939-4 I). The MilitaryAcademy represented an ingenious move by the Ministryof Defence to obtain much-needed buildings by proposing their initial use by competitors at the I940 Helsinki Olympic Games. The Games were deferredbut the MilitaryAcademy got its buildings. Confidence notesThe paper discusses Finnish military architecture 1926-1939 with specific case studies in Helsinki (Motor Transport Company barracks 1934-35, Military Hospital 1934-36, Santahamina Military Academy 1939-41). While Helsinki is mentioned as a location for specific military buildings, the paper's primary focus is on national-level military architectural policy rather than Helsinki neighbourhoods specifically. Helsinki is treated as a case site for military infrastructure rather than as a neighbourhood-scale urban study. No specific Helsinki neighbourhoods (like Kallio, Punavuori, etc.) are identified as primary subjects.
